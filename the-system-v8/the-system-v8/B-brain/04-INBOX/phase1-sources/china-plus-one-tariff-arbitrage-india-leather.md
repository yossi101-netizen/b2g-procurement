## The Structural Event That Created the Opportunity

In 2018-2019, the United States Trade Representative (USTR) implemented Section 301
tariffs on Chinese imports — additional duties layered on top of standard US MFN
rates, authorised under Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974, citing China's
unfair trade practices and intellectual property theft.

The tariffs were implemented in four lists:
- **List 1 (July 2018):** 25% additional tariff on $34B of Chinese goods
- **List 2 (August 2018):** 25% additional tariff on $16B of Chinese goods
- **List 3 (September 2018):** Initially 10%, raised to 25% in May 2019 — $200B of goods
- **List 4A (September 2019):** 15% additional tariff, covering consumer goods including
  leather handbags, wallets, and accessories under HS Chapter 42

For US-market leather goods brands, this meant overnight Chinese-origin leather
goods became 15-25% more expensive at the border. A handbag that cost $40 landed
duty-inclusive at $44 (10% MFN + 15% Section 301 = 25% additional on a simplified
basis) was now landing at prices that compressed retail margins below sustainability.

India is not subject to Section 301. Indian-origin goods pay standard US MFN rates.
For leather goods, the US MFN rate is relatively low — making India-origin leather
goods significantly cheaper to land in the US than equivalent China-origin goods.

## The "China Plus One" Strategy: What Brands Are Actually Doing

"China Plus One" is the operational decision by a brand or retailer to maintain
China manufacturing for some volume while adding a second manufacturing country
to de-risk the China concentration. The goal is not to exit China completely —
that is a multi-year supply chain transformation. The goal is to have an
established, operational alternative that can be scaled if China conditions
deteriorate further.

For leather goods brands, the typical China Plus One sequence:

1. **Year 1-2:** Pilot 20-30% of volume in the alternative country (India, Vietnam,
   Turkey, Portugal depending on product and price tier). Test quality, lead times,
   and supply chain reliability.

2. **Year 2-3:** Scale the alternative to 40-50% if pilots pass. Begin reducing
   China concentration.

3. **Year 3+:** Depending on tariff environment, brand positioning, and ESG
   pressures, potentially invert the split — 30% China, 70% India.

The primary blocker in this sequence is not finding a factory in India — it is
establishing a trusted, quality-controlled supply chain that can match or exceed
the operational reliability of an established China relationship. This is the
exact problem managed manufacturing is designed to solve.

## Why India Is the Primary China Alternative for Leather Specifically

India has structural advantages for leather goods that other "plus one" markets
lack:

**Raw material proximity:** India is one of the world's largest cattle populations,
making it a major hide producer. Domestic hide supply means Indian tanneries
are not subject to the same raw material import dependency that affects Vietnam
or Bangladesh leather manufacturers. Lower raw material logistics cost translates
to more competitive FOB pricing.

**Tannery cluster depth:** The three major Indian leather clusters — Kolkata (West
Bengal), Chennai (Tamil Nadu), and Kanpur (Uttar Pradesh) — have decades of
tannery infrastructure. LWG-certified tanneries capable of producing full-grain,
corrected-grain, and nubuck leathers are established and audited. This is not
an emerging cluster building capability from zero.

**Manufacturing cluster depth:** Chennai's Ambur and Vaniyambadi regions, and
Dharavi in Mumbai, have large-scale leather goods manufacturing infrastructure.
Factories capable of running 500-5,000 unit orders with professional sampling
and QC departments exist and are operational.

**DCTS and trade policy alignment:** India-origin goods benefit from UK DCTS
preferential duty rates. EU-India trade negotiations are ongoing with the
potential for further preferential access. US MFN rates apply without Section 301
penalties. The trade policy environment across all three major UK/EU/US markets
favours India origin over China origin.

**EUDR risk profile:** India is classified as standard-risk (not high-risk) under
the EUDR's country benchmarking system. China is not a significant cattle-hide
source, so this regulation primarily affects South American origins — not a
direct China vs. India comparison point, but India's lower EUDR compliance burden
is an additional structural advantage over Brazilian or other high-risk origins.

## The Tariff Stack: What It Actually Costs to Source from China vs. India

For a UK brand importing leather handbags:

**China-origin:**
- UK MFN rate for HS 4202.21 (leather handbags): ~3.7%
- No UK-China preferential arrangement
- ESG risk premium: Xinjiang cotton supply chain scrutiny has increased due
  diligence pressure across Chinese supply chains broadly
- Additional procurement cost: EUDR does not apply to Chinese cattle hides
  directly, but Chinese goods sold in the EU must still comply — creating
  documentation burden

**India-origin:**
- UK DCTS Standard Preferences rate: reduced from MFN
- EUDR risk profile: standard-risk, lower compliance documentation burden
- No Section 301 (US market)
- UK-India FTA in negotiation — directional improvement in preferential access

The duty differential alone is not why brands move to India. It is one of five
compounding factors: duty savings + Section 301 avoidance (US) + EUDR lower
risk + ESG narrative alignment + supply chain concentration de-risking.
Combined, these make the India sourcing case structurally compelling even if
Indian FOB pricing is 5-8% higher than Chinese FOB pricing on equivalent products.

## The MOQ Comparison: Where China Genuinely Has an Advantage

The one area where China's manufacturing infrastructure genuinely outperforms India
for small and mid-size brands is minimum order quantity (MOQ) for commodity-quality
products. Large Chinese factories are optimised for 3,000-10,000 unit runs with
highly standardised product. At that scale, their operational efficiency is
difficult to match.

For brands in the 300-3,000 unit bracket — what KritiKaal calls The Missing Middle —
Chinese large-scale factories are often not the right option anyway. They either
won't take the order (too small) or will deprioritise it (too much management
overhead relative to their large volume customers). This is the bracket where
Indian managed manufacturing operates competitively.

Indian manufacturers in the 300-3,000 unit range:
- More willing to take custom designs and prototyping work
- More flexible on material sourcing (don't require client to use their standard
  material catalogue)
- Lower setup and tooling costs for hardware and fittings
- More accessible for in-country QC inspection (costs $200-400/day for a qualified
  inspector vs. the same in China, but travel time from UK is comparable)

## The De-Risking Imperative Beyond Tariffs

COVID-19 (2020-2022) exposed the systemic risk of single-country supply chain
concentration. Factories in Guangdong and Zhejiang shut down for months. Brands
with 100% China sourcing had no production options. Brands with dual-country
operations shifted volume to their alternative supply chain and maintained delivery.

Geopolitical risk has not diminished since COVID. Taiwan Strait tensions, US-China
trade policy escalation, and the general strategic competition between Western
governments and China have all increased the political risk premium on China-heavy
supply chains.

For UK brands specifically: post-Brexit trade policy is being designed by a
government that is explicitly building trade relationships outside China's orbit.
The UK-India FTA, the CPTPP accession, and the UK's Indo-Pacific tilt all
directionally reinforce the India sourcing case.

A brand that has not established an operational India supply chain by 2026 will be
reactive to tariff or regulatory changes rather than positioned ahead of them.
The cost of reactive supply chain pivoting is always higher than the cost of
proactive establishment.

## What "Established" Actually Means for an India Supply Chain

Establishing an India leather supply chain is not placing one test order. It means:

1. A qualified manufacturer relationship where the factory knows your quality
   standards, your brand requirements, and your inspection protocol.

2. A tannery relationship (or verified tannery chain) that can provide EUDR-
   compatible documentation and LWG audit data.

3. A logistics partner familiar with UK customs documentation, REX certification,
   and the specific HS code classifications for your product range.

4. An in-country quality control capability — either an employed QC agent,
   a managed manufacturing partner who provides this as standard, or a verified
   third-party inspection company with leather goods expertise.

5. A lead time model that accounts for Indian factory capacity (typically 60-90
   days ex-factory for custom orders) and builds buffer into seasonal buying cycles.

None of these take less than 6-12 months to establish correctly. Brands that
start this process in 2025 are positioned for 2026 delivery reliability.
Brands that wait for a tariff shock to force the move will be establishing it
under operational pressure with less leverage and more mistakes.
