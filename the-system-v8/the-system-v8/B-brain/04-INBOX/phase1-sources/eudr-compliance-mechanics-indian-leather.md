## What EUDR Is and Why Leather Is Directly Affected

EU Regulation 2023/1115 — the European Union Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) —
was published in the Official Journal of the European Union on June 29, 2023. It
prohibits placing certain commodities and derived products on the EU market unless
the operator can demonstrate they were produced on land that was not deforested
after December 31, 2020.

The seven primary commodities covered are: cattle, cocoa, coffee, palm oil, soy,
wood, and rubber. Leather is a cattle-derived product. Any leather good — handbag,
wallet, belt, luggage, shoe upper — that crosses an EU border after the enforcement
date must be accompanied by a Due Diligence Statement (DDS) proving its cattle
supply chain is deforestation-free.

This is not a labelling requirement. It is a market access requirement. No DDS,
no legal sale in the EU.

## Enforcement Timeline

The original enforcement date was December 30, 2024. Following lobbying from
industry groups and diplomatic pressure from major exporting countries, the EU
Commission announced a 12-month extension. The revised dates are:

- **Large operators and traders:** December 30, 2025
- **Micro-enterprises and SMEs:** June 30, 2026

These deadlines are firm. The EU has indicated no further extensions will be
granted. Brands sourcing leather from India or any other country that plan to sell
into the EU market — directly or through UK intermediaries — must begin building
their DDS capability now. The documentation system takes 12-18 months to establish
correctly across a full supply chain.

## The Due Diligence Statement (DDS): What It Contains

The DDS is a formal declaration submitted by the operator (the brand placing the
product on the EU market) via the EU's TRACES NT information system. It must contain:

1. **Description of goods:** HS code, product description, quantity, country of
   production.

2. **Geolocation data:** GPS coordinates at plot level for the land where the cattle
   were raised. This is not country-level or region-level data. It is polygon-level
   coordinates showing the specific parcels of land. For large-scale Brazilian
   ranching operations, this is relatively structured. For Indian cattle farming —
   which is predominantly smallholder, fragmented, and poorly documented — this
   presents a significant practical challenge.

3. **Proof of no deforestation:** The geolocation data is cross-referenced against
   the EU's satellite monitoring system. Parcels flagged as having undergone
   deforestation or forest degradation after December 31, 2020 fail the check
   automatically.

4. **Supplier chain traceability:** The full chain must be documented: farm or
   grazing land → slaughterhouse → hide dealer → tannery → component supplier
   → finished goods manufacturer → brand/operator.

5. **Risk assessment:** The operator must also submit a risk assessment covering
   country risk, commodity risk, and product risk. India is currently not on the
   EU high-risk country list for cattle deforestation, which is a structural advantage
   over some South American origins.

## The LWG Pathway for EUDR Compliance

The Leather Working Group (LWG) is the primary international audit body for
responsible leather production. LWG certification — awarded at Platinum, Gold,
Silver, or Audited (Bronze) levels — evaluates tanneries on environmental
performance, traceability, and chain of custody.

LWG is developing an EUDR-compatible compliance pathway that integrates with
its existing audit framework. Key elements of the LWG-EUDR bridge:

- **Mass Balance Traceability:** LWG is implementing a system that tracks hide
  volume from slaughterhouse through tannery to finished goods with a mass balance
  approach — ensuring the weight of hides entering the system matches the weight of
  leather and leather products exiting it.

- **Geolocation Data Collection:** LWG is working with tannery members to collect
  and validate farm-level geolocation data for their hide suppliers. This is the
  single hardest operational requirement for Indian tanneries.

- **Digital DDS Preparation:** LWG's audit reports will eventually contain the
  data fields required for a DDS, allowing brands to pull their DDS data from the
  LWG system rather than collecting it independently.

For UK/EU brands sourcing leather goods from India, working with LWG-certified
tanneries is not sufficient for EUDR compliance — but it is the most reliable
starting point. An LWG Gold or Platinum tannery has already passed the
environmental audit bar; the remaining gap is the farm-level geolocation data.

## What This Means Operationally for UK Brands

A UK brand sourcing leather bags from an Indian manufacturer faces the following
compliance chain:

1. The manufacturer must source leather from a tannery that has farm-level
   geolocation data for its hide suppliers.

2. The tannery must be able to produce this data on request, in a format the brand
   can submit via TRACES NT.

3. The brand's sourcing contact in India (whether a sourcing agent, factory, or
   managed manufacturing operator) must be able to facilitate this documentation
   chain. An agent who cannot name their tannery supplier cannot provide a DDS.

4. The DDS must be submitted before the goods cross the EU border — not after.
   Customs hold for missing DDS costs approximately £500-1,500 per day in warehouse
   storage fees, in addition to potential fines.

## Why India Has a Structural Advantage Over Other Origins

India does not have tropical rainforest in its primary cattle-rearing regions.
The main cattle populations are in Uttar Pradesh, Rajasthan, Gujarat, and
Maharashtra — dryland agricultural regions with low deforestation risk. India is
currently classified as a standard-risk (not high-risk) country under the EUDR's
country benchmarking system.

Brazil — historically the dominant source of cattle hides for global luxury
leather — is classified as high-risk. Tanneries sourcing Brazilian hides face
higher scrutiny and more stringent documentation requirements. This is creating a
structural shift in tannery sourcing toward African and Asian origins, including India.

For UK brands, this means Indian-origin leather carries a lower EUDR compliance
burden than Brazilian-origin leather. The operational challenge is documentation
depth, not geographical risk.

## The Cost of Non-Compliance

The EUDR specifies penalties to be determined by member states, but requires them
to be "effective, proportionate and dissuasive." The Regulation explicitly
references penalties that can reach up to 4% of the operator's total annual EU
turnover. For a UK brand with £5 million in EU-market annual revenue, this is a
potential £200,000 fine per violation.

The EUDR also allows for temporary prohibition from the EU market for repeat
violations — effectively a market access ban. For any brand with meaningful EU
distribution, this is an existential business risk.

## Immediate Action for UK Brands Sourcing From India

1. **Map your tannery supply chain.** If your Indian manufacturer or agent cannot
   name the tannery that produced your leather, you cannot build a DDS. This is
   the first gate.

2. **Prioritise LWG-audited tanneries.** Gold or Platinum certification indicates
   the tannery has the operational discipline to work toward geolocation data collection.

3. **Request farm-level data now.** The December 2025 deadline means you need to
   run a compliance test on at least one full order before January 2025 to identify
   gaps and correct them.

4. **Audit your India partner's data readiness.** A managed manufacturing operator
   with direct tannery relationships can produce this documentation chain. A sourcing
   agent who works through intermediaries typically cannot.
