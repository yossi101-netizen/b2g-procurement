## What DCTS Is and Why It Exists

The UK Developing Countries Trading Scheme (DCTS) replaced the EU Generalised System
of Preferences (GSP) — which the UK inherited on accession and maintained temporarily
post-Brexit — on June 19, 2023. The DCTS is the UK's independent preferential trade
framework for developing countries. It determines what duty rates apply to goods
imported into the UK from qualifying countries.

Before DCTS, the UK operated under the UK GSP (an interim copy of the EU GSP).
The DCTS is materially more generous than its predecessor: it covers more countries,
extends zero-duty treatment more broadly, and consolidates the tiered structure
into a cleaner framework.

## The Three DCTS Tiers

**Enhanced Preferences (EP):** The most generous tier, applied to Least Developed
Countries (LDCs) as defined by the UN. LDC-origin goods receive zero duty on
virtually all products, including leather goods. Applicable countries include
Ethiopia, Bangladesh, and other LDCs where leather manufacturing is growing.

**Standard Preferences (SP):** Applied to lower-middle and some middle-income
developing countries. Goods receive a percentage reduction from the UK Most
Favoured Nation (MFN) tariff rate — often 20-35% reduction. India's leather
goods fall under this tier for most HS categories.

**DCTS Comprehensive Preferences:** A transitional category for countries in or
expecting trade agreement negotiations with the UK.

India falls under the Standard Preferences tier for the majority of leather goods
categories. This means India-origin leather goods pay a reduced duty rate — not
zero, but meaningfully below the UK MFN rate applied to countries like China.

## The Duty Arithmetic: India vs. China for Leather Goods

The UK applies Most Favoured Nation (MFN) tariff rates to countries with no
preferential arrangement. China has no preferential trade arrangement with the UK
post-Brexit. All Chinese-origin goods pay full UK MFN rates.

For leather goods under HS Chapter 42 (principal HS codes for bags, wallets,
leather accessories):

- **HS 4202.21** — Handbags with outer surface of leather or composition leather:
  UK MFN rate approximately 3.7%. Under DCTS Standard Preferences for India,
  this is reduced. Verify exact current rate at trade-tariff.service.gov.uk.

- **HS 4202.31** — Articles normally carried in pocket or handbag (wallets, key
  holders) with outer surface of leather: Similar MFN rate structure with DCTS
  reduction available for Indian origin.

- **HS 4202.91** — Other: trunks, cases, travelling bags with leather outer
  surface: MFN applies to China; DCTS reduction available for India.

On a £20,000 import order of leather handbags:
- China-origin: Pays full MFN duty — approximately £740 at 3.7%
- India-origin: Pays reduced DCTS duty — the specific reduction depends on the
  product and current schedule, but the differential is a real and compounding
  cost advantage across annual procurement volumes

For a brand ordering £200,000/year of leather goods, the annual duty saving from
India sourcing vs. China sourcing can reach £2,000-7,000 depending on product
mix and exact rates. This is free margin — not a quality compromise.

## Certificate of Origin: How India Proves DCTS Eligibility

For DCTS Standard Preferences to apply, the goods must have a valid proof of
preferential origin. The UK accepts:

**REX Declaration (Registered Exporter System):** The exporter must be registered
in the REX database maintained by the customs authority in their country. A
registered Indian exporter can self-certify origin on the commercial invoice using
the statement: "I, the undersigned, declare that the goods described in this
document originate in [India] in accordance with the rules of origin of the UK
Generalised System of Preferences."

The REX registration number must appear on the declaration. If your Indian
supplier or manufacturer is not REX-registered, they cannot issue a valid origin
declaration, and you pay full UK MFN rates even though you're sourcing from India.

**GSP Form A:** The traditional paper-based certificate of origin, issued by the
Indian customs authority (DGFT — Directorate General of Foreign Trade). India's
exporters routinely use Form A. For shipments valued at or below a threshold,
this is the standard mechanism. Above the threshold, REX is required.

**Practical implication:** When evaluating an Indian manufacturer, ask specifically:
"Are you REX-registered or can you issue Form A GSP certificates?" If neither,
you will pay UK MFN rates for every shipment, losing the entire DCTS benefit.
A managed manufacturing operator handles this documentation as standard — it is
not a premium service.

## Rules of Origin: What "Indian-Made" Actually Means

DCTS preferential treatment is not granted just because goods are shipped from
India. The goods must originate in India under the UK's Rules of Origin (ROO)
requirements. For leather goods, the relevant ROO is:

**Sufficient Transformation Rule:** The manufacturing process must create a product
that is substantively different from its inputs. For leather goods, this typically
means the leather cutting, stitching, assembly, and finishing must occur in India.
Importing cut leather panels from China and assembling them in India does not
qualify as Indian origin for ROO purposes.

**The China-washing risk:** Some manufacturers in India import Chinese-origin
leather or Chinese-cut components and perform final assembly in India. This is
RO non-compliance. The UK HMRC can retrospectively demand full MFN duty plus
interest and penalties if ROO non-compliance is discovered on audit. A managed
manufacturing service that controls its own supply chain verifies ROO compliance
as part of standard operations. An agent routing through multiple sub-suppliers
may not.

## The Post-Brexit Arbitrage Window

Pre-Brexit, UK brands importing from India paid EU GSP rates (which applied via
EU membership). Post-Brexit, the UK has constructed its own DCTS, and the UK-India
trade relationship is in active negotiation toward a formal Free Trade Agreement
(UK-India FTA). If the FTA is concluded, preferential rates on leather goods could
improve further — potentially to zero duty across the board.

The strategic implication for UK leather goods brands: The duty environment for
India-origin goods is currently favorable and directionally improving. The duty
environment for China-origin goods is static or deteriorating. Every month a brand
spends sourcing from China is a month of paying a tariff premium that their
India-sourcing competitors are not paying.

## The Compound Effect Across a Typical Brand's Annual Procurement

Consider a UK accessories brand with these annual procurement volumes:
- 2,000 leather handbags (HS 4202.21) at £35 FOB each = £70,000
- 3,000 leather wallets (HS 4202.31) at £12 FOB each = £36,000
- 1,500 leather belts (HS 4203.00) at £8 FOB each = £12,000
- Total FOB procurement: £118,000/year

At UK MFN rates (China origin): estimated £4,000-5,000/year in import duty.
At DCTS Standard Preference rates (India origin): significantly reduced.

Over five years, this differential — compounded against rising procurement volumes
as the brand scales — amounts to tens of thousands of pounds returned to margin.
This is before accounting for any Section 301-equivalent pressures or further
tariff policy changes that could make China-origin more expensive.

## What to Verify Before Your First India Shipment

1. Confirm your Indian manufacturer or managed manufacturing partner is
   REX-registered or issues Form A GSP certificates routinely.

2. Confirm your HS code classification for each product — small classification
   errors move goods into different duty rate categories.

3. Confirm your manufacturer's leather inputs are Indian-origin or qualify
   under cumulation rules — not Chinese-origin inputs assembled in India.

4. Check the current rate at trade-tariff.service.gov.uk using your specific
   HS code and "India" as the country of origin. Rates are updated; do not
   rely on rates quoted more than 12 months ago.

5. Retain all origin documentation (Form A or REX declaration) for at least
   4 years — UK HMRC can audit post-import for up to 3 years from the date
   of importation.
