## The Sourcing Agent Model: How It Is Supposed to Work

A sourcing agent in India theoretically serves as the buyer's representative on the
ground. They are supposed to identify qualified manufacturers, negotiate pricing,
coordinate sample development, manage production timelines, and ensure the buyer
receives what they ordered. In exchange, they receive a fee — either a fixed
project fee or a commission percentage of the order value.

This model works when the agent's financial interests are aligned with the buyer's
outcomes. In the India leather goods sector, they structurally are not.

## The Conflict of Interest That Poisons Every Agent Relationship

The primary economic reality of sourcing agents in India: most of them are paid
by both the buyer and the manufacturer simultaneously.

**The buyer's payment:** Typically 5-10% of order value, paid explicitly as a
"sourcing commission" or "agency fee."

**The manufacturer's payment:** A separate commission, typically 8-15% of the
factory's quoted price, paid by the factory to the agent for bringing the order.
This commission is hidden from the buyer. The factory builds it into the price
they quote to the agent, who passes it to the buyer as the "factory price."

**The result:** On a £20,000 order, the agent may be receiving £1,000-2,000 from
the buyer (explicit) and £1,600-3,000 from the manufacturer (hidden), for a total
income of £2,600-5,000 on a single order. Their financial incentive is to maximise
order value and maintain factory relationships — not to find the best quality at the
best price for the buyer.

The agent does not tell the buyer which factory they use. They tell the buyer they
have a "network of vetted factories." This protects their commission stream from
being disintermediated.

## Documented Betrayal Pattern 1: Factory Substitution

The most common, most damaging, and hardest to detect betrayal pattern.

**What happens:** The agent shows the buyer Factory A during the sourcing visit or
sends samples made at Factory A. The buyer is impressed. The purchase order is
placed. Production is routed to Factory B — a lower-quality facility that pays
the agent a higher commission, or where the agent has a financial stake.

**Why it's hard to detect:** The agent handles all communication between buyer and
"factory." The buyer never has direct contact with the production facility. The
packing slips and documentation show Factory A's name (which the agent has
arranged). The goods arrive from a different facility.

**How it surfaces:** The goods don't match the sample quality. The buyer requests
to visit the factory for a follow-up order and is told "the factory is at capacity
and doesn't allow visits." Or an independent inspection is ordered and the inspector
finds the address doesn't match the factory name on the documentation.

**The accountability gap:** There is typically no formal written contract between
the buyer and the agent specifying which factory must be used for production.
The agent's agreement simply says they will "source quality manufacturers." They
can argue Factory B meets this description.

## Documented Betrayal Pattern 2: Lost Deposit / Disappeared Agent

Standard payment terms in India leather manufacturing: 30% deposit on PO placement,
70% on pre-shipment inspection or bill of lading. The 30% deposit is intended to
cover raw material procurement and early production costs.

**What happens:** The buyer places the PO and pays the 30% deposit to the agent.
The agent forwards a portion to the factory (or claims to). Production begins, or
appears to begin. At 4-6 weeks into the lead time, communication becomes sporadic.
At 8-10 weeks, the agent stops responding. The buyer contacts the "factory" directly
using the contact details on the order — and discovers the factory either never
received the full deposit, never received the order at all, or disputes the amount
they were paid.

**Financial exposure:** On a £20,000 order, the 30% deposit is £6,000. Recovery
through legal action in India is expensive, slow, and often produces less than the
legal costs. Most buyers absorb the loss.

**The structural precondition:** This pattern only works because the buyer has no
direct factory relationship and no independently verified record of the deposit
reaching the factory. The agent is the single point of failure and the single
point of accountability — and they have chosen to remove themselves.

## Documented Betrayal Pattern 3: MOQ Inflation

**What happens:** The agent tells the buyer the factory's minimum order quantity
is 500 units for a custom leather bag. The buyer reluctantly agrees and places a
500-unit order. The actual factory MOQ is 200 units. The agent captures the
margin on the additional 300 units — either by running the 300 extra units for
stock to sell elsewhere, or by negotiating a lower factory price for 200 units
and billing the buyer for 500 at the higher per-unit cost.

**Why it happens:** Buyers rarely verify MOQ claims directly with factories. The
agent is the information intermediary, and MOQ is one of the primary levers
they use to control margin.

**Detection:** Instruct your own agent or managed manufacturing partner to provide
the factory's direct GST registration number and company name. With this, you can
independently request a factory visit or use a third-party inspection agency to
verify the factory's stated MOQ and capacity.

## Documented Betrayal Pattern 4: Quality Substitution and Material Downgrade

The agent approves the sample at Tannery A's leather (LWG Gold certified, full-grain
vegetable-tanned at £4.50/sqft). Production is run using leather from Tannery B
(uncertified, corrected-grain chrome-tanned at £2.80/sqft). The difference:
£1.70/sqft. On 500 bags each requiring 2sqft of leather: £1,700 hidden margin
captured by the agent through their factory arrangement.

**How it surfaces:** The goods arrive and the leather doesn't have the same handle,
sheen, or grain character as the approved sample. A material composition test
(available from specialist labs at £80-150 per sample) can distinguish full-grain
from corrected-grain leather and identify tanning method. Most buyers never
commission this test.

**The documentation absence:** If the purchase order does not specify leather grade,
tannery name, LWG certification status, and leather reference number, the agent
and factory cannot be held to account for substituting a different material.
"We used good quality leather" is their defence — and without a specification, it
may be legally sufficient.

## Documented Betrayal Pattern 5: Commission Harvesting on Accessories

Hardware, linings, zips, labels, packaging, and other accessories are typically
sourced separately from the leather. The agent coordinates this sourcing as part
of their service. Each accessory supplier pays the agent a commission for the
business. These commissions are never disclosed to the buyer.

**Consequence:** The agent specifies accessories from their commission-paying
suppliers, not from the best-value or best-quality suppliers. Your branded YKK zip
may have been substituted for a domestic equivalent because the domestic supplier
pays 12% commission and YKK India does not.

## The "Direct Factory" Illusion

Many buyers, having been burned by agent-mediated relationships, seek out "direct
factory" relationships in India. They attend trade shows (Lineapelle, APLF),
meet factory representatives, and believe they are now dealing directly with the
manufacturer.

In most cases, they are not. The "factory representative" at a trade show is often
a factory sales agent — an independent contractor who represents multiple factories
and is paid on commission. This is a structural agent relationship with a different
label.

True direct factory relationships require:
1. The buyer's representative physically visiting the factory and meeting the
   production management team — not the sales team
2. Independent verification of the factory's legal entity (GST registration,
   company incorporation certificate)
3. A contractual relationship directly with the factory legal entity, not
   through an intermediary
4. The factory's production manager being directly contactable by the buyer
   during production

Most UK brands sourcing from India do not have any of these four conditions met.
They believe they have a direct factory relationship and do not.

## The Managed Manufacturing Alternative: What Accountability Actually Looks Like

A managed manufacturing model differs from an agent relationship in one structural
way: the managed manufacturing operator's fee is fixed and transparent, and they
have contractual accountability for outcomes.

**The accountability stack in managed manufacturing:**

- **Single point of contact:** One named individual who owns the outcome from PO
  placement to delivery. Not a trading company passing communication through multiple
  intermediaries.

- **Factory relationship transparency:** The buyer is told which factory is being
  used, can visit the factory (with coordination), and has access to the factory's
  QC reports directly.

- **Tannery transparency:** The leather source (tannery name, LWG certification
  status, material specification) is disclosed at PO stage.

- **Fee structure:** The managed manufacturing fee is a fixed percentage or fixed
  rate disclosed upfront. There are no hidden factory commissions. The operator's
  incentive is delivering quality results that retain the client — not maximising
  hidden transaction income.

- **The Double-Back Guarantee:** In a properly structured managed manufacturing
  agreement, defective units are replaced before shipment at the operator's cost,
  or a credit equivalent to the defect value is issued. This is structurally
  impossible in an agent model — the agent has no liability for defects because
  they claim they do not manufacture goods, they only "introduce" parties.

## The Minimum Verification Test

Before placing a first order with any sourcing agent or claimed "direct factory"
in India, request these four items in writing:

1. **Factory GST number:** The Indian Goods and Services Tax registration number.
   This is public information. Verify it at the GST portal (gst.gov.in) to confirm
   the factory name and address match what you've been shown.

2. **Factory company name and registration:** The company's CIN (Corporate
   Identification Number) under the Ministry of Corporate Affairs. Verify at
   mca.gov.in.

3. **Tannery name and LWG status:** If they cannot name their leather tannery
   supplier, they cannot provide EUDR compliance documentation and they cannot
   control material specification.

4. **Production management direct contact:** The mobile number of the factory's
   production manager or head of manufacturing. If the agent will not provide this,
   you do not have a direct factory relationship — you have an agent-intermediated
   one, regardless of what they tell you.

An agent who refuses any of these four items is protecting their intermediary
position at your operational expense. A managed manufacturing operator provides
all four as standard — they have nothing to hide and accountability requires
this transparency.
