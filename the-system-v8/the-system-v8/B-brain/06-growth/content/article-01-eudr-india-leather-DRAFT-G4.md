# How India Solves EUDR Compliance for UK Leather Brands

*By Yossi Daniel, Founder & CEO, KritiKaal*

---

Your legal team has already sent the memo. The EU Deforestation Regulation applies to leather goods. Your current supply chain almost certainly does not meet it. And for small operators, the enforcement deadline is **30 June 2026**.

That is nine weeks from today.

EUDR is not an environmental aspiration. It is a trade barrier with teeth. Non-compliant leather goods cannot legally enter EU or UK markets. The penalty is not a fine you absorb. It is a shipment that does not clear customs, a contract your client cannot honour, and a brand reputation attached to a deforestation-risk supply chain.

The question every UK leather procurement director should be asking is not *whether* to comply. It is *which supply chain actually delivers the documentation*.

---

## What EUDR Actually Requires for Leather

The EU Deforestation Regulation applies to cattle hides — and therefore to every leather bag, wallet, belt, and accessory you manufacture. Three requirements must be met before goods enter the EU or UK market:

### 1. Deforestation-Free Origin
The hide must not originate from land deforested after 31 December 2020. This requires traceability from the farm or slaughterhouse — not just the tannery. A factory certificate is not enough. A tannery LWG audit is not enough on its own. The chain must be traceable to the source of the hide.

### 2. Country-of-Production Legal Compliance
The entire production process must comply with the laws of the country in which it takes place — including labour law, environmental law, and land-use law. A factory in a country with well-documented legal compliance and active social audit coverage meets this requirement by design.

### 3. Due Diligence Statement (DDS)
You must file a Due Diligence Statement with the EU system before any goods enter the market. It is a legal filing — not a declaration. Your legal team signs off on it. Your supply chain documentation must support every line of it.

Most UK brands cannot currently produce item 1. A sourcing agent who introduces a brand to a factory does not have visibility into that factory's hide procurement chain. Without an audited material flow at every stage, the DDS cannot be filed with confidence.

---

## Why India Is the Structural Answer

India's leather manufacturing clusters are positioned to meet EUDR in a way that most other sourcing geographies cannot. Three structural reasons:

### Domestic Bovine Hide Supply
India has the world's largest bovine population. The majority of hides used in Indian leather manufacturing are sourced domestically — not imported from South America or Sub-Saharan Africa, which are EUDR's primary deforestation-risk regions. A tannery in Chennai processing Tamil Nadu cattle hides has a fundamentally shorter, more auditable supply chain than a tannery sourcing South American calfskin.

### LWG Certification Covers Traceability
The Leather Working Group audit standard requires participating tanneries to document their hide sourcing. LWG Gold certification means a tannery's material flows have been externally verified. LWG-approved leather is not a marketing claim — it is an audited fact. India's established tannery clusters in Chennai and Kolkata include LWG-certified facilities actively supplying European fashion brands. *(G3 note: verify current LWG-certified tannery count in TN and West Bengal before publishing.)*

### BSCI, SA8000, and SEDEX Cover Labour Compliance
EUDR's country-of-production requirement includes labour law. Factories holding SA8000 or SEDEX/SMETA 4-Pillar audit certificates have had their compliance with Indian labour law externally verified. This satisfies EUDR's second condition — not as a workaround, but as a direct evidential answer.

The documentation stack that EUDR requires exists in India. It is assembled — not discovered — by a manufacturing partner with the right factory relationships and the process to collect it.

## Why a Sourcing Agent Will Not Get You This Documentation

Here is where most brands make a costly assumption.

A sourcing agent introduces you to a factory. The factory may hold an LWG certificate for its tannery supplier. The factory itself may hold a BSCI audit. The sourcing agent collects these documents and forwards them.

That is passive compliance. It is not a due diligence system.

EUDR does not ask whether your factory has documents. It asks whether *you* can demonstrate a specific chain: that the hides in your production run originated from non-deforested land. That requires hide-level procurement visibility — not factory-level audit certificates.

A sourcing agent does not have it. They are not embedded in the production process. They do not inspect in-process material sourcing. They do not have the contractual relationship with the tannery required to obtain the hide origin records.

This is not a criticism. It is a structural description of what a sourcing arrangement covers and what it does not.
