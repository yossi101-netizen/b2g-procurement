 What is an EUDR Due Diligence Statement (DDS)?

An **EUDR Due Diligence Statement (DDS)** is a **mandatory legal declaration** required under the EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR) before certain products—including **leather goods derived from cattle**—can be placed on or exported from the EU market. ([FoodChain Transparency][1])

In simple terms, it’s the **final “sign-off”** that your product is compliant.

#### What you are declaring when you submit a DDS

By filing a DDS, a company confirms that:

* The product is **deforestation-free** (no links to deforestation after Dec 31, 2020)
* It was **produced legally** in the country of origin
* A full **due diligence process (risk assessment + mitigation)** has been carried out
* The remaining risk is **“negligible”** ([coolset.com][2])

Without a DDS, **your goods cannot legally enter or leave the EU market**. ([FoodChain Transparency][1])

---

### Why leather goods are affected

Leather falls under EUDR because it is a **derived product of cattle**, which is explicitly listed in the regulation. ([FoodChain Transparency][1])

That means UK brands selling into the EU must trace leather back to:

* The **farm(s)** where cattle were raised
* The **exact geolocation** of those farms
* Proof that no deforestation occurred

This is where things get complex for fashion and leather supply chains.

---

### What must be included in a DDS (for leather goods)

A compliant DDS typically includes:

**1. Product data**

* Product description + HS code
* Quantity

**2. Origin & traceability**

* Country of production
* **Exact geolocation coordinates of farms**
* Production timeframe

**3. Supply chain & risk**

* Supplier mapping
* Risk assessment (deforestation, legality)
* Mitigation actions

**4. Company declaration**

* Operator details (e.g., EORI number)
* Statement of compliance and responsibility

**5. Submission**

* Filed digitally via the EU system (TRACES) before shipment ([EUDR][3])

# 🧾 1) What escrow means in this context

An escrow arrangement is when a **neutral third party (bank or licensed escrow service)** holds the buyer’s money until agreed conditions are met. ([Tally Solutions][1])

👉 This protects:

* **Buyer** → doesn’t pay upfront and risk poor quality / non-delivery
* **Manufacturer** → knows funds are secured before production starts

# 🏭 2) Step-by-step flow for a leather manufacturing order

### Step 1: Contract + escrow agreement

You (buyer) and the Indian leather manufacturer agree on:

* Product specs (material, stitching, finishing, packaging)
* Quantity & price
* Delivery timeline
* Inspection criteria

Then you sign:

* **Supply contract**
* **Escrow agreement** (defines when money is released)

---

### Step 2: Buyer deposits funds into escrow

Instead of paying the factory directly, you transfer money to the escrow account.

* Funds are **locked and verified**
* Supplier gets confirmation the money exists
  👉 This reduces risk of fake orders or non-payment ([Edvaya][2])

---

### Step 3: Production begins

The manufacturer starts production because:

* They know the funds are secured
* Payment is guaranteed once conditions are met

Sometimes:

* Escrow is **split into milestones**, e.g.

  * 30% after raw material procurement
  * 40% after production
  * 30% after final inspection

---

### Step 4: Inspection / quality check

Before shipment, conditions in the escrow agreement are verified:

Common triggers:

* Third-party inspection report (SGS, Bureau Veritas, etc.)
* Photos/videos of goods
* Buyer approval

---

### Step 5: Shipment & documents

Supplier ships goods and provides:

* Bill of lading
* Invoice
* Packing list

---

### Step 6: Escrow releases funds

Once conditions are met:

* Escrow agent releases payment to the manufacturer

If something goes wrong:

* Funds stay locked until dispute resolution

👉 This “conditional release” is the core of escrow ([cashfree.com][3])

Leather goods have **quality variability risk**, so escrow helps manage:

### Query 9
**Query text (paste verbatim):**
> "What is a Single Point of Accountability in leather goods manufacturing?"

**ChatGPT response:**
```
A **Single Point of Accountability (SPOA)** in leather goods manufacturing means **one clearly designated person is responsible for the final outcome of a product or process**, even though many people may contribute to it.

---

## 🧵 What it looks like in practice

In a leather factory—whether producing handbags, wallets, belts, or shoes—multiple stages are involved:

* Design & pattern making
* Leather cutting
* Stitching & assembly
* Finishing & polishing
* Quality control

With SPOA, **one person (often a production manager, line leader, or product owner)** is accountable for the entire chain for a specific product, order, or batch.

---

## 🎯 Why companies use SPOA

Without a SPOA, problems can fall into the classic trap of “everyone’s responsibility = no one’s responsibility.”

SPOA ensures:

* **Clear ownership** → someone is ultimately responsible for deadlines, quality, and cost
* **Faster decisions** → no confusion about who approves changes
* **Better coordination** → smoother handoffs between cutting, stitching, and finishing teams
* **Accountability for defects** → easier root-cause analysis

---

## 👜 Example in leather goods

Imagine a factory producing premium handbags:

* The **cutting team** says leather defects came from sourcing
* The **stitching team** blames cutting inaccuracies
* The **QC team** flags final defects

With SPOA:

* A **Product Line Manager** owns the entire handbag batch
* If defects appear, **they are accountable**, regardless of where the issue originated
* They coordinate fixes across teams instead of passing blame

---

## ⚖️ Important distinction

SPOA does **not** mean:

* One person does all the work
* Others are not responsible for their tasks

It means:

* **Many people are responsible for execution**
* **One person is accountable for the final result**

---

## 🧠 Simple way to remember it

> **Responsibility = who does the work**
> **Accountability = who answers for the result**
