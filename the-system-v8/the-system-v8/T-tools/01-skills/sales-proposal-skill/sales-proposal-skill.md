# Sales Proposal Skill

Write proposals that make saying yes feel obvious.

## When to Use

- Writing client proposals or offers
- Creating pricing presentations
- Drafting follow-up emails after sales conversations
- Any client-facing communication that leads to a deal

---

## Proposal Fundamentals

**What wins deals:**
- Show you understand their problem before offering a solution
- Make the ROI obvious (not just "it's worth it")
- Remove friction from the decision
- Sound like a partner, not a vendor

**Proposal length:**
- **Quick offer (email):** 200-400 words (for warm leads, simple services)
- **Standard proposal:** 1-2 pages (for most B2B services)
- **Detailed proposal:** 3-5 pages (for complex projects, enterprise)

**The rule:** Shorter is almost always better. Respect their time.

---

## The Proposal Structure

### The Winning Framework

```
1. THEIR SITUATION (not yours)
   → Show you listened. Mirror their words back.

2. THE PROBLEM (as they feel it)
   → Name the pain. Be specific.

3. WHAT CHANGES (the transformation)
   → Paint the after. Make it concrete.

4. HOW WE DO IT (your approach)
   → 3-5 clear steps. Not jargon. Not methodology names.

5. WHAT THEY GET (deliverables)
   → Specific, tangible list.

6. INVESTMENT (pricing)
   → Frame as investment, show what they get at each level.

7. NEXT STEPS (make it easy)
   → One clear action. Remove all friction.
```

## Section-by-Section Guide

### 1. Their Situation

Start with them, not you. This is where 90% of proposals fail — they open with "About us..."

```
Good:
"You mentioned that your team spends ~15 hours/week on manual reporting.
That's 780 hours/year — roughly $50K in salary costs alone."

Bad:
"We are a leading provider of automation solutions with 10 years of experience..."
```

### 2. The Problem

Name it clearly. Use their language, not yours.

```
Good:
"Right now, every new client onboarding takes 2 weeks and requires 3 team members.
That limits you to ~4 new clients per month."

Bad:
"There are inefficiencies in your current workflow."
```

### 3. The Transformation

Show what "after" looks like. Be specific.

```
Good:
"After implementation: onboarding drops to 3 days, handled by 1 person.
You go from 4 new clients/month to 12+."

Bad:
"Our solution will optimize your processes."
```

### 4. The Approach

3-5 clear steps. Plain language.

```
Phase 1: Audit (Week 1)
→ Map your current onboarding process end-to-end

Phase 2: Build (Weeks 2-3)
→ Create automated workflows + client portal

Phase 3: Launch (Week 4)
→ Train your team + go live with first 3 clients
```

### 5. Deliverables

Be specific. Ambiguity kills deals.

```
What you get:
- Custom onboarding automation (built on [platform])
- Client-facing portal with status tracking
- Team training session (recorded for future hires)
- 30 days of support post-launch
- Documentation for your team
```

### 6. Investment/Pricing

**3 Rules for presenting price:**
1. Always show what they GET, not just what they PAY
2. Anchor with value before showing the number
3. Make saying "yes" easy (clear tiers, simple terms)

```
Investment: $X,XXX

What this includes:
- [Deliverable 1]
- [Deliverable 2]
- [Deliverable 3]

ROI context: At your current cost of $X/month for manual work,
this pays for itself in [timeframe].
```

**Optional: Tiered pricing**
```
Option A: [Name] — $X,XXX
→ [Core deliverables]

Option B: [Name] — $X,XXX (Most popular)
→ [Everything in A + extras]

Option C: [Name] — $X,XXX
→ [Everything in B + premium extras]
```

### 7. Next Steps

One clear action. Make it effortless.

```
Good:
"If this looks right, reply 'let's go' and I'll send the contract today."

"Book a 15-min call to finalize details: [link]"

Bad:
"Please review the attached documentation and reach out to discuss
potential next steps at your earliest convenience."
```

## Sales Email Templates

### The Follow-Up After a Meeting

```
Subject: [Specific thing you discussed]

Hey [Name],

Great talking today. Here's what I took away:

- You need [specific outcome]
- The main blocker is [their problem]
- Timeline: [when they want results]

I put together a quick proposal (attached / below).
The short version: [one sentence summary of your offer].

Worth a look?

[Your name]
```

### The Cold Outreach (Warm Style)

```
Subject: Quick question about [their specific situation]

Hey [Name],

I noticed [specific observation about their business].

We helped [similar company] go from [before state] to [after state]
in [timeframe].

Would a 10-minute call be worth it to see if we could do the same for you?

[Your name]
```

### The Nudge (When They Go Quiet)

```
Subject: Still relevant?

Hey [Name],

I sent a proposal last [day]. Wanted to check:

1. Did it land in the right inbox?
2. Is the timing still right?
3. Any questions I can answer quickly?

No pressure. Just want to make sure you have what you need.

[Your name]
```

## Writing Rules for Proposals

### Rule 1: Their Words, Not Yours
Mirror the exact language they used in conversations. If they said "onboarding is a mess," write "onboarding is a mess" — not "onboarding optimization opportunity."

### Rule 2: Numbers Beat Adjectives
```
Bad: "Significant improvement in efficiency"
Good: "Cut onboarding from 14 days to 3 days"
```

### Rule 3: Short Sentences Win
Proposals should feel light to read, even when the topic is complex.

### Rule 4: One Clear CTA
Don't give them 5 things to think about. One next step. One action.

### Rule 5: Remove Risk
Address the "what if it doesn't work?" question before they ask it.
- Money-back guarantees
- Pilot projects
- Performance milestones
- "Start small, scale if it works"

---

## What to Avoid

1. **Opening with "About Us"** — They don't care yet. Show you understand them first.
2. **Jargon and methodology names** — Plain language builds trust.
3. **Vague deliverables** — "Strategic consulting" means nothing. Be specific.
4. **Hiding the price** — Don't make them hunt for it.
5. **Multiple decision points** — One clear next step.
6. **Over-promising** — Under-promise, over-deliver. Always.

## Quality Checklist

Before sending:

- [ ] Opens with their situation (not yours)?
- [ ] Uses their exact language?
- [ ] Problem is specific and named?
- [ ] Transformation is concrete (with numbers)?
- [ ] Approach is clear (3-5 steps, plain language)?
- [ ] Deliverables are specific and tangible?
- [ ] Price is framed with ROI context?
- [ ] One clear next step?
- [ ] Risk is addressed?
- [ ] Read it from their perspective — would you say yes?
