# Document Summary Skill

Turn chaos into clarity. Raw material in, structured insights out.

## When to Use

- Summarizing meeting recordings or notes
- Processing user interview transcripts
- Condensing research documents or reports
- Summarizing any long-form content into actionable briefs

---

## Summary Fundamentals

**What makes a great summary:**
- Captures the 20% that contains 80% of the value
- Distinguishes facts from opinions from decisions
- Surfaces action items (who, what, by when)
- Reads in under 2 minutes

**Summary length by source:**
- **30-min meeting:** 1/2 to 1 page
- **1-hour meeting:** 1 page
- **Long document/report:** 1-2 pages
- **User interview:** 1 page per interview

**The rule:** A summary should be short enough to read but long enough to act on.

---

## The Summary Framework

### Universal Summary Structure

```
# [Title]: [Topic/Meeting/Document Name]

**Date:** [Date]
**Source:** [Meeting / Interview / Document / Recording]
**Participants:** [Names, if applicable]

---

## TL;DR
[2-3 sentences capturing the essence. If someone reads ONLY this, they get it.]

---

## Key Points
1. [Most important point]
2. [Second most important]
3. [Third most important]

---

## Decisions Made
- [Decision 1]: [Context and reasoning]
- [Decision 2]: [Context and reasoning]

---

## Action Items
| Who | What | By When |
|-----|------|---------|
| [Name] | [Specific action] | [Date] |

---

## Open Questions
- [Question that wasn't resolved]
- [Topic that needs follow-up]

---

## Raw Notes / Details
[Supporting details for reference, organized by topic]
```

## Summary Types

### Meeting Summary

**Focus on:** Decisions, action items, disagreements resolved.

**Special rules:**
- Lead with decisions, not with "the meeting started with..."
- Capture WHO said what when it matters
- Action items must have owners and deadlines
- Note what was NOT decided (open questions)

```
What to capture:
✓ Decisions made and their reasoning
✓ Action items with owners
✓ Key disagreements and how they were resolved
✓ Open questions that need follow-up

What to skip:
✗ Greetings and small talk
✗ Repetitive discussion before reaching a conclusion
✗ Administrative logistics (unless they're action items)
```

### User Interview Summary

**Focus on:** User's actual words, pain points, workarounds, unmet needs.

**Special rules:**
- Preserve key quotes exactly as said
- Separate what the user SAID from what you INTERPRET
- Note emotional moments (frustration, excitement, confusion)
- Capture workarounds — they reveal unmet needs

```
# User Interview: [User Name/ID]

**Date:** [Date]
**Role:** [Their role/context]
**Duration:** [Length]

---

## Key Quotes
> "[Exact quote that captures a key insight]"
> "[Another revealing quote]"

---

## Pain Points (What hurts)
1. [Pain point] — [Their words describing it]

## Workarounds (What they do instead)
1. [Workaround] — [What this tells us]

## Wishes (What they want)
1. [Desired outcome] — [How they described it]

---

## Insights (Your interpretation)
- [Pattern or insight you noticed]
- [Connection to other interviews]

---

## Action Items
| What | Priority | Notes |
|------|----------|-------|
| [Follow-up needed] | High/Med/Low | [Context] |
```

### Research Document Summary

**Focus on:** Key findings, implications, and recommended actions.

**Special rules:**
- Lead with "so what?" — why does this matter?
- Quantify findings (numbers > adjectives)
- Note methodology limitations
- Separate findings from recommendations

```
# Research Summary: [Topic]

**Source:** [Document/Report name]
**Date:** [Date]
**Author(s):** [Original authors]

---

## Why This Matters
[1-2 sentences on relevance to your work]

---

## Key Findings
1. [Finding with specific data]
2. [Finding with specific data]
3. [Finding with specific data]

---

## Implications
- [What this means for your work]
- [What this means for your decisions]

---

## Recommended Actions
1. [Action based on findings]
2. [Action based on findings]

---

## Limitations
- [What the research doesn't cover]
- [Methodology caveats]
```

## Extraction Principles

### What to Extract (Always)
- **Facts:** Numbers, dates, names, specific claims
- **Decisions:** What was decided and why
- **Actions:** Who needs to do what, by when
- **Quotes:** Key phrases in the speaker's exact words
- **Open items:** What's unresolved

### What to Compress (Usually)
- Repetitive discussion leading to a conclusion
- Context that's already known by the reader
- Examples that illustrate the same point

### What to Skip (Almost Always)
- Small talk and pleasantries
- Tangential discussions that led nowhere
- Administrative details (unless they're action items)
- Restating things already captured

---

## Writing Rules for Summaries

### Rule 1: Lead with the Headline
Don't build up to the point. Start with it. The TL;DR is the first thing they read.

### Rule 2: Facts, Then Interpretation
```
Bad: "The team seemed frustrated with the process."
Good: "3 of 5 team members said the process takes too long (quotes below)."
```

### Rule 3: Specific Over Vague
```
Bad: "Several issues were discussed."
Good: "3 issues were raised: deployment speed, test coverage, and on-call rotation."
```

### Rule 4: Action Items Must Be Actionable
```
Bad: "Follow up on the design."
Good: "Sarah: Share updated mockups with the team by Friday."
```

### Rule 5: Preserve the Speaker's Voice
When a quote matters, use their exact words. Paraphrasing loses nuance.

## Quality Checklist

Before delivering:

- [ ] TL;DR captures the essence in 2-3 sentences?
- [ ] Key points are prioritized (most important first)?
- [ ] Decisions have context and reasoning?
- [ ] Action items have owners and deadlines?
- [ ] Open questions are captured?
- [ ] Facts are separated from interpretations?
- [ ] Reads in under 2 minutes?
- [ ] Someone who wasn't there would understand?
- [ ] Nothing important is missing?
