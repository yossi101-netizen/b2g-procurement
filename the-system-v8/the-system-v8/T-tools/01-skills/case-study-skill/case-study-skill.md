# Case Study Skill

A case study is proof. Not a story. Not a testimonial. Proof that you did the thing, for a real person, and it worked. This skill helps you turn a client win into a case study that sells your next client.

---

## When to Use

- A client project just wrapped up and you want to document the results
- You need social proof for your website, portfolio, or sales deck
- You want to turn a success story into a LinkedIn post or blog article
- A prospect asks "do you have examples of your work?"
- You're building a portfolio to attract a specific type of client

---

## Case Study Fundamentals

**What makes a case study work:**

1. **The transformation is the story.** Before vs. after. That's it. Everything else supports this.
2. **Specific beats impressive.** "Revenue increased 34% in 90 days" beats "significant revenue growth."
3. **The client is the hero.** Not you. You're the guide who helped them get there.
4. **One problem, one solution, one result.** Don't cram everything you did into one case study.
5. **Make it skimmable.** Decision-makers won't read a 2,000-word essay. They'll scan for the numbers and the outcome.

**Length guidelines:**

| Format | Length |
|--------|--------|
| LinkedIn post version | 150-200 words |
| Website case study | 400-800 words |
| Detailed portfolio piece | 800-1,500 words |
| Sales deck slide | 3-5 bullets + 1 key metric |

**The governing rule:** A case study that doesn't include a specific, measurable result is just a story. Stories are nice. Results close deals.

## The Structure

### The Core Framework: SOAR

```
S — Situation: Who was the client? What was their world before you?
O — Obstacle: What specific problem were they stuck on?
A — Action: What did you do? (Keep this brief. Focus on the approach, not every detail.)
R — Result: What changed? Show the numbers.
```

### Full Case Study Structure

```
[HEADLINE] — Result-first. "How [Client] achieved [Result] in [Timeframe]"

[SNAPSHOT] — Quick-reference box:
  Client: [Name/Industry]
  Challenge: [One sentence]
  Solution: [One sentence]
  Result: [Key metric]
  Timeline: [How long]

[SITUATION] — 2-3 sentences. Set the scene.
  Who is the client? What do they do? What was their context?

[THE PROBLEM] — 2-3 sentences.
  What specific challenge were they facing?
  What had they tried before? Why didn't it work?

[THE APPROACH] — 3-5 bullets or a short paragraph.
  What did you do differently? Why this approach?
  Keep it high-level. Don't list every task.

[THE RESULTS] — The money section.
  Lead with the biggest number/impact.
  Include 2-3 supporting metrics.
  Use before/after comparisons when possible.

[CLIENT QUOTE] — Optional but powerful.
  One sentence from the client in their own words.

[KEY TAKEAWAY] — One sentence.
  What's the lesson someone else can learn from this?
```

---

## Headlines That Work

**Result-first pattern:**

| Formula | Example |
|---------|---------|
| How [Client] achieved [Result] in [Time] | How Acme SaaS achieved 3x pipeline growth in 90 days |
| From [Before] to [After]: [Client]'s story | From 2% conversion to 11%: Buildify's landing page overhaul |
| [Number]: How [Client] [achieved outcome] | 340% ROI: How TechFlow cut onboarding time in half |

**Don't use:**
- "Case Study: Our Work with Acme Corp" (boring, no result)
- "A Success Story" (generic, could be anyone)
- "How We Helped a Client" (who? with what?)

---

## Writing the Results Section

This is the most important part. Get it right.

**Show the transformation:**

This:
> **Before:** 2% demo-to-close rate, average deal cycle of 47 days
> **After:** 8.5% demo-to-close rate, average deal cycle of 22 days
> **Impact:** Revenue increased 34% in the first quarter after implementation

Not this:
> We significantly improved their sales performance and the client was very satisfied with the results.

**Use real numbers whenever possible:**

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Improved engagement" | "Engagement increased from 2.1% to 6.8%" |
| "Faster delivery" | "Delivery time dropped from 3 weeks to 4 days" |
| "Better retention" | "Churn dropped from 8% to 2.3% monthly" |
| "More leads" | "Lead volume increased 240% in 60 days" |

**When you can't share exact numbers:**

- Use percentages: "47% increase in qualified leads"
- Use ranges: "Response time improved from days to hours"
- Use multiples: "3x more demo requests"
- Ask the client what you can share before publishing

## Adapting by Format

### LinkedIn Post Version

```
[Hook: The result or transformation — one punchy line]

[1-2 sentences of context: who, what problem]

[What we did: 2-3 bullets MAX]

[The result: specific numbers]

[One-line takeaway or lesson]

[CTA: Want similar results? Link/DM]
```

### Sales Deck Slide Version

```
[Client Logo/Name]
[Industry — Company Size]

Challenge: [One sentence]
Solution: [One sentence]
Result: [One BIG number]

"[Short client quote]"
```

### Website Version

Use the full SOAR structure. Add:
- Client logo (with permission)
- A snapshot/summary box at the top for scanners
- Visuals if available (before/after screenshots, charts, graphs)

---

## Writing Rules

1. **Lead with the result, not the client description.** The reader wants to know: "Does this person deliver results?" Answer that first.

2. **Keep the "what we did" section short.** Your prospect cares about outcomes, not your process. 3-5 bullets. That's it.

3. **Use the client's language.** If they said "we were drowning in spreadsheets," write that. Not "they faced data management challenges."

4. **Include one quote.** Even a short one. It adds credibility nothing else can.

5. **Make numbers visual.** Use before/after comparisons, bold the key metrics, use percentage changes.

6. **Name the industry and company size.** Prospects want to see someone like them. "B2B SaaS, 50 employees" helps them self-select.

---

## What to Avoid

1. Making yourself the hero instead of the client
2. Vague results ("great improvement," "very satisfied")
3. Including every detail of what you did (the full project timeline is not interesting)
4. Publishing without the client's permission
5. Using stock photos instead of real screenshots or data
6. Writing a case study for a project that didn't have measurable results (use a different format instead)

---

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] The headline includes a specific result or transformation
- [ ] There's a clear before/after comparison
- [ ] At least one specific, measurable result is included
- [ ] The "what we did" section is 5 bullets or fewer
- [ ] The client is the hero, not the author
- [ ] Industry and company context are clear
- [ ] There's a client quote (or a note explaining why not)
- [ ] It's scannable (headers, bold metrics, snapshot box)
- [ ] Zero em dashes
