# Research Briefing Skill

Turn any topic into a clear, actionable one-page briefing.

## When to Use

- Researching competitors or market trends
- Summarizing a complex topic for decision-making
- Creating executive summaries
- Analyzing an industry, technology, or opportunity
- Preparing background for a meeting or strategy session

---

## Briefing Basics

**What makes a good briefing:**
- Answers a specific question (not "tell me everything about X")
- Leads with the conclusion (not builds up to it)
- Uses numbers, not adjectives
- Ends with "here's what to do," not "here's what exists"

**Length sweet spot:** 400-600 words
- Too short: Missing critical context
- Too long: Nobody reads it
- Perfect: One page, scannable in 2 minutes

---

## The Briefing Structure

Every briefing follows the same 5-section format:

### Section 1: Executive Summary (2-3 sentences)
```
The key finding in plain language.
What the reader needs to know right now.
Why it matters to their specific business.
```

This is the most important section. If someone reads nothing else, they get the point.

### Section 2: Key Findings (3-5 bullets)
```
1. [Finding] — [Number or specific detail]
2. [Finding] — [Number or specific detail]
3. [Finding] — [Number or specific detail]
```

Each finding is one sentence. Each has a number or specific detail. No fluff.

### Section 3: Analysis (1-2 paragraphs)
```
What this means for the business.
Connections between findings that aren't obvious.
What's changing and why it matters.
```

This is where you add interpretation. Label opinions clearly.

### Section 4: Recommendations (2-3 actions)
```
1. [Do this] — [Because of finding X]
2. [Do this] — [Because of finding Y]
3. [Consider this] — [If condition Z is true]
```

Concrete. Specific. Tied back to the findings.

### Section 5: Sources & Limitations
```
- Where the data came from
- What you couldn't verify
- What to watch for next
```

Honesty about what you know and what you don't.

## Research Approach Templates

### Template 1: Competitor Analysis
```
Executive Summary: [Who they are, how they compare, your advantage]

Key Findings:
1. Their positioning: [what they say vs. what they actually do]
2. Their audience: [who they serve, overlap with yours]
3. Their pricing: [specific numbers]
4. Their weakness: [what they're missing]
5. Their strength: [what they do well]

Analysis: [What this means for your strategy]

Recommendations: [Specific moves to make]
```

### Template 2: Market/Trend Analysis
```
Executive Summary: [What's happening, why it matters now]

Key Findings:
1. Market size/growth: [numbers]
2. Key players: [who's doing what]
3. Customer behavior shift: [what changed]
4. Technology enabler: [what made this possible]

Analysis: [Where this is going in 6-12 months]

Recommendations: [How to position for this trend]
```

### Template 3: Decision Briefing
```
Executive Summary: [The decision at hand, your recommendation]

Key Findings:
1. Option A: [pros, cons, numbers]
2. Option B: [pros, cons, numbers]
3. Risk factors: [what could go wrong]

Analysis: [Why one option is stronger]

Recommendations: [Go with X because Y]
```

### Template 4: Industry Overview
```
Executive Summary: [State of the industry, key opportunity]

Key Findings:
1. Market size: [numbers]
2. Growth drivers: [what's pushing it]
3. Key challenges: [what's holding it back]
4. Emerging opportunities: [where the gaps are]

Analysis: [What this means for someone entering/competing]

Recommendations: [Where to focus]
```

## Writing Rules

### Rule 1: Lead With the Conclusion
Don't make the reader wait. First sentence = the answer.

```
Bad: "After analyzing 12 competitors in the wellness coaching space..."
Good: "You have 3 direct competitors. None of them combine astrology with business strategy."
```

### Rule 2: Numbers Over Adjectives
```
Bad: "The market is growing significantly"
Good: "The market grew 34% YoY to $2.1B"
```

### Rule 3: Label Your Certainty
```
Verified: "Revenue was $4.2M in 2025 (from their annual report)"
Estimated: "Revenue likely $3-5M based on team size and pricing"
Signal: "Three job postings suggest they're expanding into enterprise"
```

### Rule 4: Make Recommendations Specific
```
Bad: "Consider entering this market"
Good: "Test with a landing page targeting Series A founders in Q2. Budget: $500 for ads. Success metric: 50 signups in 2 weeks."
```

---

## What to Avoid

1. **"Data dumps"** — listing everything you found without analysis
2. **Burying the lead** — putting the important stuff at the end
3. **No recommendations** — research without "so what" is a Wikipedia article
4. **Vague sources** — "according to reports" means nothing
5. **Over-promising** — if you're not sure, say so
6. **Jargon** — write for a smart person who isn't an expert in this topic

---

## Quality Checklist

Before delivering:

- [ ] Executive summary stands alone (reader gets the point without reading further)?
- [ ] At least 3 specific numbers in key findings?
- [ ] Facts clearly separated from opinions?
- [ ] Recommendations are actionable (who, what, when)?
- [ ] Sources listed (or limitations acknowledged)?
- [ ] Scannable in 2 minutes (headers, bullets, short paragraphs)?
- [ ] Relevant to the brand's specific situation (not generic)?
